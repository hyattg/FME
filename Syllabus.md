## Course Description

Field Microbial Ecology is an intensive, immersive field course that introduces students to the ecology of microorganisms in natural environments. Working at and around Cranberry Lake Biological Station in the Adirondacks, students investigate the diversity and functional roles of bacteria, archaea, fungi, and protists across a range of habitats, including lakes, streams, wetlands, soils, and leaf litter. The course integrates classical microscopy and culture-based methods with modern molecular and bioinformatic approaches. Students design and execute independent field sampling campaigns, process environmental samples in the on-site laboratory, and analyze and interpret ecological datasets using R.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

1. Describe the major groups of microorganisms and their functional roles in terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems.
2. Design and execute rigorous field sampling protocols to address ecological questions about microbial communities.
3. Apply classical (microscopy, culturing, staining) and molecular (DNA extraction, PCR, amplicon sequencing) methods to characterize microbial diversity.
4. Process and analyze microbial community data (amplicon sequence variants, diversity metrics, ordination) using R and standard bioinformatic pipelines.
5. Interpret results in the context of ecological theory (succession, niche partitioning, biogeography, nutrient cycling).
6. Communicate scientific findings through written reports and oral presentations.

## Course Schedule

The course runs for approximately two weeks. The schedule below is a typical outline; daily activities may shift due to weather, equipment availability, or emergent scientific findings.

### Week 1 — Foundations and Field Sampling

| Day | Morning | Afternoon / Evening |
|-----|---------|---------------------|
| 1 | Orientation; station safety; introduction to Cranberry Lake ecosystems | Overview of microbial diversity; microscopy lab — direct counts and staining |
| 2 | Aquatic sampling: lake pelagic zone (bacteria, phytoplankton) | Filtration and preservation; introduction to R and data management |
| 3 | Aquatic sampling: littoral zone and wetland sediments | DNA extraction from water and sediment samples |
| 4 | Terrestrial sampling: forest soils and leaf litter | Soil microbiology — respiration assays, dilution plating |
| 5 | Stream biofilm and epilithic microbial mats | Culture isolation; streak plating; colony morphology |
| 6 | Student-designed sampling expedition | Evening: PCR amplification (16S rRNA, ITS) |
| 7 | Rest / free exploration | Optional: microscopy of isolated cultures |

### Week 2 — Molecular Methods, Analysis, and Synthesis

| Day | Morning | Afternoon / Evening |
|-----|---------|---------------------|
| 8 | Gel electrophoresis; amplicon QC; library preparation overview | Introduction to amplicon bioinformatics (DADA2 / QIIME2 concepts) |
| 9 | R workshop: DADA2 pipeline; denoising and taxonomy assignment | R workshop: phyloseq — building and exploring community objects |
| 10 | Alpha diversity: richness, Shannon, Faith's PD | Beta diversity: ordination (PCoA, NMDS); PERMANOVA |
| 11 | Nutrient cycling and functional ecology; metagenomics overview | Connecting community patterns to environmental gradients |
| 12 | Independent project analysis and writing | Peer data clinic — troubleshooting analyses |
| 13 | Student presentations (10 min each + Q&A) | Course synthesis discussion; wrap-up |
| 14 | Depart |  |

## Course Policies

### Attendance

Because this is a residential field course with a condensed schedule, attendance at all field excursions, laboratory sessions, and group activities is **mandatory**. Missing a session without a documented medical or emergency reason will result in a deduction from the participation grade and may affect your ability to complete required assignments. Please communicate with the instructor as early as possible if a conflict arises.

### Field Safety

- All students must complete the station safety orientation on Day 1 before participating in any field activity.
- The buddy system is required at all times in the field — never sample alone.
- Students must inform the instructor of any allergies, medical conditions, or mobility limitations before the course begins.
- Appropriate footwear (closed-toe shoes or rubber boots) and personal protective equipment (nitrile gloves, eye protection) are required in the laboratory.
- Swimming and watercraft use are subject to station rules and must be approved by station staff.

### Academic Integrity

All written work must be your own. Collaboration on data analysis and interpretation is encouraged and should be acknowledged, but written reports must be independently composed. Use of generative AI to produce report text is not permitted unless explicitly authorized by the instructor. Violations will be handled according to the SUNY ESF Academic Integrity Policy.

### Accessibility and Accommodations

Students requiring accommodations should contact the SUNY ESF Office of Accessibility Services and provide documentation to the instructor at least one week before the course begins. We are committed to making field and laboratory activities as accessible as possible and will work with students individually to identify appropriate accommodations.

### Communication

The primary means of communication during the course is in person. For urgent matters when away from the station, contact the instructor by email. Response time may be limited in areas with poor cell/internet coverage.
