About the Course

Most microbiology happens in a lab. This course flips that: you start at the field site — an iron seep, a nitrogen-fixing alder stand, a methane-producing wetland — and work backward to the bench. Every sample gets processed two ways: direct environmental observation and classical pure-culture isolation. By the end of 10 days, you'll understand why these two approaches tell different stories about the same community.

The emphasis is on doing real science, not following recipes. You'll develop hypotheses, design sampling schemes with proper controls, collect and analyze data in R, and present your findings to your peers.

What You'll Do

🔬

Iron-Oxidizing Bacteria

Visit iron seeps, measure geochemistry (pH, O2, temp), and examine iron-oxidizer morphology under the microscope.

🌳

Nitrogen Fixation

Survey Speckled Alder and Sweetgale stands, excavate root nodules, and characterize Frankia symbioses across soil gradients.

🌊

Water Quality

Sample Cranberry Lake using Van Dorn bottles, run coliform and heterotrophic plate counts, and assess microbial water quality.

🔥

Methanogenesis

Collect anoxic muck, capture methane in balloons, test flammability, and connect decomposition to anaerobic metabolisms.

🧫

Antimicrobial Resistance

Plate environmental samples on antibiotic media, enumerate resistant colonies, and run cross-streak inhibition assays.

📊

Data Analysis & Communication

Analyze your results in R, prepare figures, and present your findings in both written and oral formats.

FAQ

Do I need microbiology experience?

No. The course has no prerequisites. If you've taken Gen Bio I & II, you have enough background. We'll teach you the lab and field techniques on site.

What's the day off for?

July 12 is unscheduled. Explore the Adirondacks, rest, catch up on notes, or go for a swim in Cranberry Lake.

Will I use a computer?

Yes. You'll have internet access and will use R for data analysis. But the heart of the course is fieldwork and benchwork, not screens.
